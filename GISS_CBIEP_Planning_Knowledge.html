Purpose About This Document

What this document does

Provides a shared, consistent framework for writing meaningful CBIEP goals for students transitioning into Grade 8. It reduces workload for feeder teachers, improves IEP usefulness for secondary staff, and aligns practices with established GISS CBIEP language and expectations.

Teachers describe the student. The agent writes the goals. Teachers are not expected to write goal language — they provide anecdotal, descriptive information about the student as a learner.

How to use this tool

  1. Provide a description of the student's strengths, needs, learning barriers, and what has helped in the past
  2. Include anecdotal information for the rationale: how the student experiences learning, interacts with others, and responds to stressors
  3. The agent generates: Goal · Rationale · Objective · Strategies · Measurement · Essential Supports
  4. Review the output and request any adjustments
  5. Copy and paste directly into the IEP
Non-Negotiable Guardrails — Apply to Every Section of Every Goal

Language & Framing

  • Strength-based and asset-oriented language
  • Reflective and respectful tone — always strengthen student dignity
  • Growth-focused, not productivity- or compliance-focused
  • Inclusive and dignity-preserving language
  • No timelines or deadline language
  • No diagnostic or medical terminology

Rationale Rules

  • Goal Bank rationale = foundation only, not final output
  • Must be expanded using teacher-provided student information
  • Minimum 4–5 sentences using the student's name
  • Reference student's strengths, interests, or preferences
  • Describe how the access need shows up in learning contexts
  • Explain why the goal supports engagement, access, and wellbeing

Objective Rules

  • Use "I can" statements
  • Name one concrete, specific strategy — not a category
  • If organizational: name a specific Microsoft suite tool
  • If regulation: name one concrete classroom-appropriate strategy
  • No vague generalizations ("agreed-upon strategy/system")
  • No category labels instead of actual strategies

Strategies Rules

  • Written for secondary teachers in a 4-block-per-day schedule
  • Elaborate on the concrete strategy named in the objective
  • Include easy-to-follow guidelines for teaching, implementing, monitoring
  • Check-ins must be described as unobtrusive
  • Be practical and specific to the secondary context

Measurement — Required Three-Sentence Structure

1
"We will know that [student name] has met this objective when the student reports [specific growth in the area of the objective]."
2
"Progress will be measured through [one or more means below]."
teacher observation report cards attendance student feedback feedback from supporting adults other meaningful measures
3
"Triangulated evidence regarding progress towards this objective will be recorded in the GISS CBIEP Feedback database."
Context Core Grade 8 Needs Patterns Informing the Goal Bank

Analysis of CBIEP goals and rationales shows Grade 8 students commonly need support in these areas. Each pattern represents an access issue, not a diagnosis.

Accessing learning when expectations are unclear or open-ended
Initiating tasks and maintaining engagement
Managing executive function demands across multiple classes
Regulating emotional and cognitive load in busy environments
Building independence without withdrawing supports
Participating in collaborative learning
Developing confidence and willingness to take learning risks
Goal Bank Grade 8 CBIEP Goal Bank — 7 Entries

Select the entries that best match the student's profile. Personalise all sections using teacher-provided information. Goal text must be used as written.

ENTRY 1 Accessing Learning Through Clarity and Communication Personal & Social

Goal

I can recognize my strengths and use strategies that help me access my learning and support my well-being.

Foundation Rationale

Grade 8 classrooms are less predictable and more complex. Clear expectations and embedded strategies support engagement and understanding when learning feels unclear.

Foundation Objective

I can recognize when instructions or tasks feel unclear and communicate what would help me get started.

Foundation Strategies

    Supportive adults will:
  • Use clear, direct language when introducing tasks
  • Break tasks into manageable entry points
  • Provide models or examples
  • Check in unobtrusively
  • Normalize clarification
    • The student will:
  • Notice when expectations feel unclear
  • Use language or tools to communicate needs
  • Reflect on helpful strategies
ENTRY 2 Task Initiation and Sustaining Engagement Thinking

Goal

I can approach learning tasks in ways that help me begin, continue, and complete my work.

Foundation Rationale

Large or multi-step tasks can delay engagement. Structured entry points support persistence and access to learning.

Foundation Objective

I can use step-by-step strategies to help me begin and continue my work.

Foundation Strategies

    Supportive adults will:
  • Provide step lists
  • Support identification of a first step
  • Check in during work periods
  • Reinforce effort
    • The student will:
  • Use steps to begin tasks
  • Communicate when steps feel unclear
  • Reflect on what helps sustain focus
ENTRY 3 Managing Executive Function Demands Thinking

Goal

I can organize my learning and use strategies that help me manage multiple tasks and expectations.

Foundation Rationale

Multiple classes and routines increase cognitive load. Organizational strategies support clarity and confidence.

Foundation Objective

I can use organizational strategies to keep track of my learning and next steps.

Foundation Strategies

    Supportive adults will:
  • Chunk information
  • Support prioritization
  • Use visual or written planning tools
  • Check in around next steps
    • The student will:
  • Use tools to track tasks
  • Communicate when expectations feel hard to manage
  • Reflect on successful strategies
ENTRY 4 Emotional Regulation for Learning Access Personal & Social

Goal

I can recognize how my feelings affect my learning and use strategies that help me stay engaged.

Foundation Rationale

Busy environments increase cognitive and emotional load. Regulation strategies support access and well-being.

Foundation Objective

I can notice when I am feeling overwhelmed and use strategies to re-engage.

Foundation Strategies

    Supportive adults will:
  • Maintain predictable routines
  • Normalize regulation strategies
  • Offer quiet check-ins or space
    • The student will:
  • Notice signs of overwhelm
  • Use agreed-upon strategies
  • Reflect on what helps
ENTRY 5 Developing Independence While Maintaining Supports Personal & Social

Goal

I can take part in my learning in ways that help me grow in independence while using strategies that work for me.

Foundation Rationale

Independence does not require removal of supports. Embedded strategies maintain access and confidence.

Foundation Objective

I can use strategies and supports that help me participate and make progress.

Foundation Strategies

    Supportive adults will:
  • Embed supports into routines
  • Normalize strategy use
  • Support gradual independence
    • The student will:
  • Use available strategies
  • Reflect on supports that help participation
ENTRY 6 Participating in Learning with Peers Communication

Goal

I can participate in learning with others in ways that feel manageable and supportive.

Foundation Rationale

Collaborative tasks increase social demands. Structured support enables participation and access.

Foundation Objective

I can take part in collaborative learning using strategies that help me stay engaged.

Foundation Strategies

    Supportive adults will:
  • Clarify roles and expectations
  • Provide structured entry points
  • Allow flexible participation
    • The student will:
  • Use agreed strategies to engage
  • Communicate when collaboration feels challenging
ENTRY 7 Building Confidence and Taking Learning Risks Personal & Social

Goal

I can approach learning with confidence and try strategies even when I am unsure.

Foundation Rationale

Fear of mistakes can limit participation. Emphasizing growth supports engagement.

Foundation Objective

I can attempt learning tasks using strategies even when I am not fully confident.

Foundation Strategies

    Supportive adults will:
  • Normalize mistakes as learning
  • Break tasks into smaller steps
  • Offer reassurance and feedback
    • The student will:
  • Attempt tasks using strategies
  • Reflect on effort and progress
Essential Supports GISS Essential Supports — Select Verbatim by Category

Select applicable items word-for-word. Organise by category in the output. Base selection on the full student profile and best practices from the BC Ministry of Education K-12 Reporting Order, Special Education Manual, BC Curriculum, inclusive education, and UDL.

Assessment & Response
  • Extended time and/or reduced assessment volume without altering curricular intent
  • Flexible methods to demonstrate learning aligned with curricular competencies (e.g., oral, visual, digital, structured formats)
  • Use of triangulated assessment (observation, conversation, product) to determine learning progress
  • Assessments provided in digital format when available
  • Recognition-based assessment formats used when appropriate (e.g., multiple choice, matching, short response)
  • Assessments chunked into manageable sections
  • Access to a separate or quiet setting for assessments when required
  • Assistive technology permitted during assessments (e.g., text-to-speech, speech-to-text, spell check, calculator as appropriate)
  • Spelling, handwriting, and transcription errors not penalized unless directly assessed
  • Additional processing time provided before requiring responses
  • Alternatives to whole-class verbal responses (e.g., written, digital, individual check-in)
Content & Learning Process
  • Instruction delivered in clear, manageable chunks with explicit transitions
  • Key content front-loaded through notes, outlines, exemplars, or preview materials
  • Explicit instruction paired with modelling, guided practice, and feedback
  • Reduced cognitive load during instruction (minimizing simultaneous listening, copying, and processing)
  • Access to notes, slide decks, or outlines to reduce note-taking demands
  • Use of visual supports (graphic organizers, step lists, worked examples, anchor charts)
  • Learning framed using big-picture concepts before focusing on detail
  • Opportunities for choice in learning activities and response formats
  • Structured supports for written output (pre-writing organizers, sentence starters, rubrics, exemplars)
  • Reduced reading demands and/or alternate access to content (audio, video, summaries) when appropriate
  • Explicit support interpreting graphs, charts, diagrams, and symbolic representations
  • Multisensory learning opportunities (visual, verbal, hands-on, digital)
  • Ongoing formative feedback to support progress and reduce anxiety
Self-Management & Organization
  • Clear expectations communicated both verbally and in writing
  • Large tasks and projects broken into explicit steps with interim deadlines
  • Use of checklists, planners, or digital tools to support task initiation and completion
  • Support with time management within a rotating block schedule
  • Reminders and check-ins related to deadlines and missing work
  • Support to organize materials (digital and/or physical)
  • Opportunities for movement and pacing, including short breaks as needed
  • Explicit instruction and coaching in executive functioning skills
  • Reduced simultaneous task demands (e.g., listening while writing or organizing)
Instruction & Representation
  • Clear, concise instructions using consistent and straightforward language
  • Information presented using multiple representations (visual, verbal, written, modelled)
  • Big-picture frameworks introduced before complex or abstract detail
  • Key instructions repeated and paraphrased as needed
  • Modelling and worked examples provided before independent practice
  • Visual supports used for abstract or language-heavy concepts
  • Frequent, unobtrusive checks for understanding
Social & Emotional Supports
  • Classroom environment that normalizes mistakes as part of learning
  • Predictable routines and clear expectations across learning contexts
  • Positive reinforcement focused on effort, strategy use, and progress
  • Environmental adjustments (e.g., preferential seating) to reduce distraction
  • Opportunities for student self-reflection and goal-setting
  • Access to calming strategies during independent work as needed
  • Coordinated communication among staff when concerns arise
Technology & Accessibility
  • Access to assistive technology as required (text-to-speech, speech-to-text, spell check, calculator)
  • Use of computer or personal device for written work
  • Learning materials provided in accessible digital formats when possible
  • Digital submission of assignments when appropriate
  • Access to Ministry-approved resources (e.g., ARCBC) when applicable
  • Consistent use of school-wide learning platforms to support organization
